# Professional Shot Breakdown Guide

Use this guide when the script is approved and the user asks for a professional storyboard, shot list, or shot breakdown.

## Shot Breakdown Purpose

A shot breakdown is not a prompt list. It is a directing document that defines what must be seen, how attention moves, what performance must happen, how the edit will work, and what production risks must be managed.

## Shot Breakdown Template

```markdown
# Professional Shot Breakdown

## Director's Shooting Approach
- Visual strategy:
- Camera rhythm:
- Performance approach:
- Editing principle:
- Sound principle:

| Shot | Time | Shot Size | Camera / Movement | Frame Content | Blocking / Action | Performance Note | Dialogue / Text | Sound | Edit Logic | Production Notes |
|---:|---:|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
```

## Shot Size Vocabulary

Use precise terms where helpful:

- Extreme wide shot
- Wide shot
- Full shot
- Medium wide shot
- Medium shot
- Medium close-up
- Close-up
- Extreme close-up
- Insert shot
- Over-the-shoulder
- Point-of-view
- Two-shot
- Tracking shot
- Static locked-off shot

## Camera / Movement Vocabulary

- Static
- Slow push-in
- Pull-back
- Pan
- Tilt
- Dolly
- Handheld
- Gimbal follow
- Whip pan
- Rack focus
- Match cut setup
- Hidden cut setup

## What Each Shot Must Clarify

- Why this shot exists.
- What the audience should notice first.
- What changes during the shot.
- What performance detail matters.
- What sound or edit cue supports the shot.
- What continuity risk exists.

## Coverage Notes

After the table, include:

```markdown
## Continuity Notes

## Coverage Notes

## Production Risks

## Optional Alternate Shots
```

Keep optional alternates short and practical.
